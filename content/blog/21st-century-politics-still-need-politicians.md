A few weeks ago, Prime Minister Mark Carney took the floor at the Liberal convention.

"AI, artificial intelligence, the defining technology of our era, is arriving," he said. "The question is not whether AI will transform our lives. It will. The question is whether it will improve the lives of all Canadians or benefit only some."

As with many of Carney's speeches, this one hit all the right notes. "Our goal is AI for all," he continued. "AI governed by Canadian values, AI accountable to Canadians, AI that serves Canadians."

But I want to ask you a question…do you believe him?

Do you think this political system — not just this Prime Minister, or this government, but this *system* — is actually capable of ensuring AI benefits everyone?

My guests today don't think so.

Hélène Landemore is a political theorist and the author of a new book called *Politics Without Politicians*, where she argues that electoral politics is no longer capable of delivering good governance — and maybe never was. And she thinks it should be citizens like you who get to call the shots, not politicians.

That model is sometimes known as deliberative democracy, and it often takes the form of something called a "citizen's assembly" — which Peter MacLeod has been pioneering in Canada for twenty years. His new book is *Democracy's Second Act: Why Politics Needs the Public*.

The conversation you're about to hear isn't really about AI. But it is about whether our current form of politics is capable of governing it. Or whether a radical new technology requires an equally radical form of governance.

At a moment when AI is threatening our jobs, our planet, and what it means to be human…could part of the solution be giving more power back to people?
