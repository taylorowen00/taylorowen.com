Ottawa is moving to restrict children's access to social media, and now plans to extend the same logic to AI chatbots. I joined Front Burner today to make the case for a different approach.

First, any age-based restriction only works if there is an independent regulator behind it. Australia enacted its under-16 ban in December because the eSafety Commissioner had a decade of operational capacity. Canada has no equivalent infrastructure. A standalone ban here would punish users rather than the products causing the harm, and offer no protection the moment a child crosses the age threshold. This is one of the questions we have been working through on the Minister's online harms expert panel, and it keeps arriving at the same place: the regulator is the precondition, not an afterthought.

Second, the difference between a ban and a moratorium is the whole argument. As Helen Hayes and I argued in the Globe earlier this year, governments should treat any restriction as a temporary moratorium tied to platform reform, not a permanent ban. A moratorium signals that access will be restored once platforms meet specific, measurable design standards: limits on algorithmic amplification, the disabling of features engineered for compulsive use, independent audits of recommendation systems. It shifts the burden back onto the companies. A permanent ban accepts that digital public space has been surrendered to corporate interests, and chooses exclusion over reform.

Third, on chatbots: caution. Tumbler Ridge exposed the regulatory vacuum in concrete terms, and the case for bringing consumer-facing AI into the Online Harms Act is strong. The question is not whether to do this, but how. Folding chatbots into a prohibition rather than a moratorium would repeat the original mistake. These products are optimized for engagement in ways that produce predictable harms, and prohibition leaves the design untouched.

The Online Harms Act, reintroduced and amended to bring chatbots into scope, is the right architecture. A standalone ban is not.
