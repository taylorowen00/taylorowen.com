It's looking increasingly like Ottawa is moving to restrict children's access to social media, and is now considering extending the same logic to AI chatbots. I joined Jayme Poisson on Frontburner today to wrestle with some of the complexity of these ideas. A few overarching thoughts:

First, any age-based restriction only works if there is an independent regulator behind it. Australia enacted its under-16 ban in December because the eSafety Commissioner had a decade of operational capacity. Canada has no equivalent infrastructure. A standalone ban here would punish users rather than the products and companies causing the harm, it offers no protection the moment a child crosses the age threshold, and it assumes that these products can never be made safer, running counter to the core premise of the Online Harms Act and all other regulatory regimes we have seen work in other countries. This is one of the questions we have been working through on the government's expert panel on online safety (more on that soon), and I keep arriving at the same place: the independent regulator is the precondition, not an afterthought.

Second, there is a critical distinction between a ban and a moratorium. As Helen Hayes and I argued in the Globe earlier this year, governments should treat any restriction as a temporary moratorium tied to platform reform, not a permanent ban. A moratorium signals that access will be restored once platforms meet specific, measurable design standards as outlined in the Duty to Protect Children component of the Online Harms Act. This shifts the burden back onto the companies, where it belongs. A permanent ban accepts that digital public space has been surrendered to corporate interests, that it can never be made safe, and chooses exclusion over reform.

Third, on chatbots: a caution. Tumbler Ridge exposed the regulatory vacuum in concrete terms, and the case for bringing consumer-facing AI into the Online Harms Act is strong. They should be subject to a separate set of obligations designed specifically for chatbots under the Duty to Act Responsibly and the Duty to Protect Children. However, folding chatbots into a prohibition, rather than a potential moratorium would repeat the original mistake. There is no question that there are real harms designed into these systems, the question is how we can best incentivize companies to ensure they are safe before they are deployed in Canada, and to hold them accountable when they are not.

The Online Harms Act, reintroduced and amended to bring chatbots into scope, with a moratorium on kids access to social media and possibly chatbots, is the right architecture. A permanent or standalone ban are not.
